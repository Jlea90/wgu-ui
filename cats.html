Cats were first domesticated around 7500 BCE in the western Asia region and are currently the second most popular domestic pet in the United States. With over 60 documented breeds, care and diet can vary. However, certain feeding practices are generally recommended for the well-being of your fluffy family member regardless of breed.

Zero to Four Weeks

During this stage, it is crucial that the queen (the term for a female cat that is pregnant or nursing) directly nurses her young. Monitor your kitten’s growth to ensure a steady growth rate. If a kitten isn’t gaining weight sufficiently, supplemental feeding via a bottle or feeding tube may be necessary.

  • Too many siblings competing for mom's milk
  • Gastrointestinal disease
  • Extreme environmental conditions or unsanitary conditions

Four Weeks to One Year

Around three to four weeks after birth, kittens can begin transitioning to soft wet kitten food. The ASPCA recommends that kittens in this age range eat half to one cup of dry kitten food or six to nine ounces of wet kitten food per day. If hard food proves challenging, add a little water to soften it. Foods formulated for kittens supply the extra nutrients needed for rapid growth and energy.

One Year to Seven Years

Once cats mature, their activity levels and metabolism decrease. It is best not to leave food out all day. Instead, serve meals at designated times to prevent constant snacking, which can help reduce the risk of obesity and related health issues.

Seven Years and More

In later years, a cat’s body begins to change, requiring fewer calories and fats, but more quality proteins. When shopping for senior cat food, look for products that specify a particular protein source—such as "salmon"—to ensure you're providing high‑quality nutrition.
